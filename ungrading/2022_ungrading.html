Ungrading for the First Time

In the summer of 2022, I had the pleasure of teaching Boston University's Linear Algebra course (MA 242). As linear algebra is a favorite topic of mine and BU's summer courses have little oversight for graduate student instructors, I found myself in an ideal opportunity to try my hand at ungrading. Debra Borkovitz had exposed me to the world of alternative assessment in the previous semester and I had since found myself gravitating toward the idea of stripping away grades from the classroom to accentuate learning. Ungrading (also known as self-assessment) and the specific issues it seeks to address especially resonated with my frustrations both as an instructor and as a student. While the preparation for the course was quite stressful at times, I absolutely loved every day of teaching and I cannot undersell the positive effect ungrading had on my students. Below is a somewhat detailed description of how I set up my course, my reasoning for each component, and some reflections as an instructor.

Philosophy and Goals in Structure

Before dumping the details of implementation, I think it will be helpful to denote my personal philosophy and goals in teaching. The two guiding principles I had in mind was to respect students' personal timeline in learning, to minimize the stigma of grades, and to promote self-awareness.

Each student has their own timeline in learning. While courses provide a framework for the collective, there is sure to be massive individual variance in the speed with which course material fully sinks in. By avoiding grades until the very end, students have room to invest their time by reflecting on their strengths and weaknesses without the jeopardy of losing points in the long run.

The added benefit of eliminating regular grading is the removal of labels on student work. I grew up in a household that stressed the importance of doing well academically. While there were some benefits to this environment, my biggest regret in my earlier schooling is the tunnel-vision I adopted in getting the highest possible grade. This had side-tracked any true learning and incentivized shortcuts and strategies to game the academic system. Furthermore, getting suboptimal grades on assignments had massive effects on my psyche and hindered me from taking productive steps to address why I had gotten said grade. While grades are often seen as a way to help students gain context for their progress, my experience on the opposite end of grades leads me to believe the negative effect of regular grades actively undermine the positive effects they could have.

Lastly, my main goal in this course was to impart self-awareness in students, both academically and socially. If a student walked out of my class and forgot all the material but retained the ability of assess their own understanding and progress, I would consider my job a massive success. (Though, the mathematician in me would be slightly sad.) While courses serve to impart certain skills and concepts, in reality, most of these skills can be done by computers in this day and age. Especially for a topic like linear algebra, the vast majority of students will never run calculations by hand once the course is over (and in some sense, they shouldn't). In response, I wanted to shift the focus of the class toward conceptual understanding of the theory and ideas underlying linear algebra.

On paper, this steers further away from practicality. However, this brought a new air to the classroom since a course on theory meant discourse and discussions. My class needed to provide a space for students to formulate and verbalize their understanding and synthesize the material to and with their peers. I wanted to create an environment where students can constantly analyze their own understanding and ask questions to their peers and myself to further explore the topics at hand. As displayed below, my approach for this was to put self-reflection at the center of the classroom and challenge my students to stress-test their ideas. Especially with the absence of grades, I found that students had the room to be wrong and the peace to be unashamed of their errors. Instead of hiding their mistakes from me and their peers, everyone could air out their confusions and take appropriate steps to gain better and deeper understanding.

Assignments

There were two assignments each week: Problem Sets and Reflections. Problem sets were traditional lists of exercises pertaining to the week's material. Most of these were taken from the book, but I always included a few extra problems which were more abstract and verging on concepts outside the scope of the class. However, these problems were optional.

For submitted problems, I did not provide letter, numerical, nor symbolic grades; I only provided feedback. This was to reflect studies which have shown that grades on individual assignments steered students away from reading any feedback by pulling the focus towards the provided label. Explicitly, I tried not to provide solutions and instead pointed out errors or provided leading questions to steer students towards identifying their own misunderstandings. In general, I did not emphasize any minor calculation errors as long as the errors did not allude to a lapse in understanding.

Reflections were free-form write-ups meant for students to reflect on the week's material and their progress in the class. The rough guideline I provided was to address three things in each write-up. The first was to respond to my feedback on the previous week's problem set. Students tended to list out what their errors were and either provide corrections along with why they had made a mistake or ask questions regarding the feedback with their understanding of what was incorrect. The second guideline was to address feedback for previous reflections. These served to continue the conversation from earlier reflections. The third was to provide any revelations, connections, or corrections the students made in the past week. This facet of reflections was the true core of meta-cognition in the course. It served to give students a chance to piece previous material together and verbalize the progress they were constantly making. Especially since class usually ended with challenging problems, I noticed that students would regularly solve problems they had previously given up on to demonstrate their newfound understanding.

Lectures

It feels like a misnomer to call my classes lectures. Each session usually started with a warm-up activity. These would serve multiple purposes such as review of previous material, computational practice, and/or reframing of established material. Most importantly, though, I would include a teaser for the day's topic. For example, I would have students do two seemingly different computations which give the exact same answer. The students would then be asked to make some predictions about why this phenomenon is taking place. My usual template in creating these activities was to ask students to create a vector from a random choice of numbers (perhaps with restrictions to avoid complications) and to copy down their neighboring classmate's vector. By using these two vectors as the seed for problems, students could compare their calculations and end up working together in troubleshooting and discussing.

After the warm-up activity, I usually lectured for about an hour to relay the actual material of the day. My personal approach to these monologues was to start from extremely natural and simple questions. For example, to arrive at the concept of a determinant, I started with a quick review of linear independence of vectors and bases. To address the question of detecting when a collection of vectors form a basis, I steered the discussion towards a geometric approach which led to the concept of a parallel piped. To find the volume of this piped, I had to introduce a way to finding heights via dot products. I then asked students to try finding the area of the piped formed by two vectors in R^2 which organically created the determinant of 2x2 matrices. The rest of the lecture was exploring what we can determine (pun unintended) from this new concept and tying everything back to the question of detecting a basis. This created a natural narrative for students to follow and appreciate. Furthermore, by encouraging students to think about these concepts as stories arising from the simplest or pre-established concepts in the course, I found that students gained a level of literacy and perspective that a traditional list of facts could not hope to impart.

To close each session off, I put a rather difficult problem on the board that the students were encouraged to work on together. Sometimes, these problems were asking students to recreate a calculation I had performed during the lecture, but often, I would try to pose these problems in a slightly removed fashion. As an example, after covering a change of basis, I asked students to find me a matrix that will rotate R^3 90 degrees about the line x=y=z. We had covered what rotations would look like about a coordinate axis in a previous class, but the idea was for students to synthesize their understanding of bases, geometric transformations, and change of bases to solve this problem. While many students were dumbfounded by the challenge at first, over the course of 30 minutes, after much discussions among smaller groups and experimentation, many students were well on their way to solving the problem. These problems also had the added benefit of making everybody lost. While this may sound bad on paper, it helped foster the idea that everybody in the class had room to grow. Even if some students were more outspoken during lectures or had less feedback on their assignments, these problems provided a common ground of perplexity.

One thing I should mention regarding the closing problems is the frustration students had in not being able to immediately solve problems. To make sure students felt validated in their frustrations, I always made sure to include a comment or two about difficult problems. Namely, if a problem was complicated, I gave the disclaimer that it probably won't be a simple calculation. Another strategy I implemented was to jolt the students away from getting lost. I find it extremely easy to lose oneself in calculations, so when most students were looking lost or defeated, I asked everyone to put down their writing utensils, find a partner or two, and discuss their ideas without writing anything down. This had the effect of students taking students out of the microcosym of their own minds to verbalize and listen to an external party. While students quickly returned to their own calculations, I've noticed the general confidence growing after these small jolting exercises.

Assessment

Ideally, I would not have assigned any letter grade at the end of the course, but the university required this of me. To provide this letter grade, I implemented two conferences to reflect and discuss progress with students. [FINISH]
